## Pythagorean Triple

A **Pythagorean triple** consists of three positive integers$$a $,$$b $, and$$c$$that satisfy the equation$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2 $. A common example is$$(3, 4, 5) $. If$$(a, b, c)$$is a Pythagorean triple, then$$(ka, kb, kc)$$for any positive integer$$k$$is also a Pythagorean triple. Triangles with side lengths that form a Pythagorean triple are right triangles and are called Pythagorean triangles.

A **primitive Pythagorean triple** is one where$$a $,$$b $, and$$c$$are coprime (i.e., they have no common divisor larger than 1). For instance,$$(3, 4, 5)$$is primitive, while$$(6, 8, 10)$$is not. Any Pythagorean triple can be reduced to a unique primitive Pythagorean triple by dividing$$(a, b, c)$$by their greatest common divisor. Conversely, any Pythagorean triple can be generated by multiplying a primitive Pythagorean triple by a positive integer.

The name "Pythagorean triple" derives from the Pythagorean theorem, which states that the side lengths of any right triangle satisfy$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2 $. Thus, Pythagorean triples describe the integer side lengths of right triangles. Right triangles with non-integer sides do not form Pythagorean triples.

### Generating Pythagorean Triples

**Euclid's Formula** is a fundamental method for generating Pythagorean triples. Given two integers$$m$$and$$n$$where$$m > n > 0 $, the formula:

$$a = m^2 - n^2, $

$$b = 2mn, $

$$c = m^2 + n^2 $

produces a Pythagorean triple. For example, setting$$m = 2$$and$$n = 1$$yields the primitive triple$$(3, 4, 5) $.

A triple generated by Euclid's formula is primitive if$$m$$and$$n$$are coprime and exactly one of them is even. If both$$m$$and$$n$$are odd,$$a $,$$b $, and$$c$$will be even, and the triple will not be primitive. However, dividing$$a $,$$b $, and$$c$$by 2 will yield a primitive triple when$$m$$and$$n$$are coprime.

Every primitive triple can be obtained from a unique pair of coprime numbers$$m$$and$$n $, one of which is even. Consequently, there are infinitely many primitive Pythagorean triples. This relationship is referenced throughout discussions on Pythagorean triples.

Euclid's formula can be extended to generate all Pythagorean triples by introducing an additional parameter$$k $:

$$a = k(m^2 - n^2), $

$$b = k(2mn), $

$$c = k(m^2 + n^2) $

where$$m $,$$n $, and$$k$$are positive integers,$$m > n $, and$$m$$and$$n$$are coprime and not both odd.

This formula generates all Pythagorean triples uniquely. By verifying$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$through algebra, we can confirm that every Pythagorean triple can be reduced to its primitive counterpart and multiplied by an integer$$k $.

Selecting$$m$$and$$n$$from specific integer sequences can yield interesting results. For example, choosing$$m$$and$$n$$as consecutive Pell numbers makes$$a$$and$$b$$differ by 1. Many other formulas for generating triples with specific properties have been developed since Euclid's time.

### Elementary Properties of Primitive Pythagorean Triples

### General Properties

1. For a primitive Pythagorean triple $(a, b, c)$$with $a < b < c$:
   - The expression $\frac{(c-a)(c-b)}{2}$$is always a perfect square. This is a necessary but not sufficient condition for a Pythagorean triple. For instance, $(6, 12, 18)$$and $(1, 8, 9)$$pass this test but are not Pythagorean triples.
   - If $(a, b, c)$$forms a primitive Pythagorean triple, then $(c - \text{even leg})$$and half of $(c - \text{odd leg})$$are perfect squares. Again, this is not a sufficient condition, as seen with $(1, 8, 9)$, which is not a Pythagorean triple since $1^2 + 8^2 \neq 9^2$.
   - At most one of $a, b, c$$is a square.
   - The area of a Pythagorean triangle cannot be a perfect square or twice a perfect square.
   - Exactly one of $a$$or $b$$is even, and $c$$is always odd.
   - Exactly one of $a$$or $b$$is divisible by 3, but never $c$.
   - Exactly one of $a$$or $b$$is divisible by 4, but not $c$.
   - Exactly one of $a, b, c$$is divisible by 5.
   - The largest number that always divides $abc$$is 60.
   - Any odd number of the form $2m + 1$, where $m > 1$, can be the odd leg of a primitive Pythagorean triple. Only even numbers divisible by 4 can be the even leg.
   - The hypotenuse $c$$is the sum of two squares, requiring all prime factors to be of the form $4n + 1$, making $c$$also of the form $4n + 1$.
   - The area $K = \frac{ab}{2}$$is a congruent number and divisible by 6.
   - For any Pythagorean triangle, the radii of the incircle and the three excircles are integers. For a primitive triple, the radius of the incircle is $r = n(m - n)$, and the radii of the excircles opposite $m^2 - n^2$, $2mn$, and $m^2 + n^2$$are $m(m - n)$, $n(m + n)$, and $m(m + n)$, respectively.
   - The diameter of the circumcircle equals the hypotenuse; hence, for primitive triples, the circumdiameter is $m^2 + n^2$$and the circumradius is $\frac{m^2 + n^2}{2}$, which is rational but non-integer.
   - The area of a Pythagorean triangle multiplied by the curvatures of its incircle and three excircles yields four positive integers satisfying Descartes's Circle Equation.
   - Only two sides of a primitive Pythagorean triple can be prime.
   - No primitive Pythagorean triangle has an integer altitude from the hypotenuse, meaning they are indecomposable.
   - The set of all primitive Pythagorean triples forms a rooted ternary tree.
   - Neither of the acute angles in a Pythagorean triangle can be a rational number of degrees.

### Special Cases

1. Every integer greater than 2 that is not congruent to 2 mod 4 is part of a primitive Pythagorean triple. For 4k, set $n = 1$$and $m = 2k$; for $2k + 1$, set $n = k$$and $m = k + 1$.
2. Every integer greater than 2 is part of a primitive or non-primitive Pythagorean triple.
3. There are infinitely many Pythagorean triples where the hypotenuse and the longest leg differ by exactly one. These are necessarily primitive.
4. There are infinitely many primitive Pythagorean triples where the hypotenuse and the longest leg differ by exactly two.
5. There are infinitely many Pythagorean triples where the two legs differ by exactly one.
6. For any positive integer $k$, there are $k$$Pythagorean triples with different hypotenuses but the same area.
7. For any positive integer $k$, there are at least $k$$different primitive Pythagorean triples with the same leg $a$.
8. For any positive integer $k$, there are at least $k$$different Pythagorean triples with the same hypotenuse.
9. If $c$$is a prime power $p^e$, there exists a primitive Pythagorean triple $a^2 + b^2 = c^2$$if and only if the prime $p$$is of the form $4n + 1$.
10. A positive integer $c$$is the hypotenuse of a primitive Pythagorean triple if and only if each prime factor of $c$$is congruent to 1 modulo 4.
11. There are infinitely many Pythagorean triples where both the hypotenuse $c$$and the sum of the legs $a + b$$are square numbers.
12. There exist non-primitive Pythagorean triangles with an integer altitude from the hypotenuse. These are known as decomposable since they can be split along the altitude into two separate and smaller Pythagorean triangles.

## Jacobi–Madden

The Jacobi–Madden equation is a specific Diophantine equation of the form:

$$a^4 + b^4 + c^4 + d^4 = (a + b + c + d)^4 $

where $a$, $b$, $c$, and $d$$are integers. This equation was discovered by John H. Conway and Richard K. Guy in 2002. The equation represents a notable result in the study of Diophantine equations, showcasing an unexpected relationship between fourth powers.

An example solution to the Jacobi–Madden equation is:

$$30^4 + 120^4 + 272^4 + 315^4 = 353^4 $

Here, $a = 30$, $b = 120$, $c = 272$, and $d = 315$, all of which satisfy the equation when summed on both sides.

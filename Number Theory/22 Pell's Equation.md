# Pell's Equation

Pell's equation, denoted as $ x^2 - ny^2 = 1 $, where $ n $ is a positive integer that is not a perfect square, seeks integer solutions for $ x $ and $ y $. This equation is named after John Pell due to a historical attribution mistake by Leonhard Euler.

**Historical Background**

Pell's equation has roots dating back to ancient times, notably in Indian and Greek mathematics. Brahmagupta in the 7th century found solutions for specific cases such as $ x^2 - 92y^2 = 1 $. Solutions to Pell's equation are integral to approximating square roots using rational numbers.

Here is a detailed and clearer explanation of finding the fundamental solution of Pell's equation using continued fractions:

## Fundamental Solution via Continued Fractions

Pell's equation, $ x^2 - ny^2 = 1 $, where $ n $ is a non-square positive integer, has a fundamental solution $ (x_1, y_1) $. This solution minimizes $ x $ among all solutions where $ x $ and $ y $ are integers. The key to finding this fundamental solution lies in the sequence of convergents $ h_i/k_i $ to the regular continued fraction for $ \sqrt{n} $.

**Sequence of Convergents**

The regular continued fraction of $ \sqrt{n} $ is represented as $ [a_0; a_1, a_2, \ldots] $, where $ a_i $ are integers. This sequence is unique and eventually periodic, written in the form:

$ [a_0; a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_{p-1}, 2\lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor \overline{a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_{p-1}}] $

Here, $ \lfloor \sqrt{n} \rfloor $ is the integer part of $ \sqrt{n} $, and $ \overline{a*1, a_2, \ldots, a*{p-1}} $ denotes the periodic part that repeats indefinitely. Notably, the sequence $ (a*1, a_2, \ldots, a*{p-1}) $ is palindromic, meaning it reads the same forwards and backwards.

**Finding the Fundamental Solution**

The fundamental solution $ (x_1, y_1) $ of Pell's equation corresponds to specific terms in this continued fraction sequence:

$
(x_1, y_1) = \begin{cases}
(h_{p-1}, k_{p-1}), & \text{for } p \text{ even} \\
(h_{2p-1}, k_{2p-1}), & \text{for } p \text{ odd}
\end{cases}
$

where $ h_i/k_i $ are the convergents and $ p $ is the index of the convergent in the sequence.

**Computational Complexity**

The process of finding the fundamental solution using continued fractions, assisted by algorithms like the Schönhage–Strassen algorithm for fast integer multiplication, operates within a logarithmic factor relative to the size of the solution's digits $ (x_1, y_1) $. However, this method is not polynomial-time because the number of digits in the solution can grow as large as $ \sqrt{n} $, which exceeds polynomial growth in terms of input size $ n $.

## Additional Solutions from the Fundamental Solution

Once the fundamental solution $ (x_1, y_1) $ of Pell's equation $ x^2 - ny^2 = 1 $ is found, all other integer solutions $ (x_k, y_k) $ can be algebraically calculated using the formula:

$ x_k + y_k \sqrt{n} = (x_1 + y_1 \sqrt{n})^k $

Expanding the right-hand side and equating coefficients of $ \sqrt{n} $ on both sides yields the recurrence relations:

$ x*{k+1} = x_1 x_k + n y_1 y_k $
$ y*{k+1} = x_1 y_k + y_1 x_k $

These relations allow for the computation of successive solutions $ (x*{k+1}, y*{k+1}) $ based on the previously computed solution $ (x_k, y_k) $.

## Concise Representation and Faster Algorithms

While the fundamental solution $ (x_1, y_1) $ can involve very large numbers when expressed explicitly, it can often be represented more compactly using smaller integers $ a_i $, $ b_i $, and $ c_i $ in the form:

$ x*1 + y_1 \sqrt{n} = \prod*{i=1}^{t} (a_i + b_i \sqrt{n})^{c_i} $

This form uses much smaller integers compared to the direct binary representation of $ (x_1, y_1) $.

**Example: Archimedes' Cattle Problem**

For instance, Archimedes' cattle problem, which is equivalent to the Pell equation $ x^2 - 410286423278424y^2 = 1 $, has a fundamental solution with 206545 digits if written out explicitly. However, it can also be represented as:

$ x_1 + y_1 \sqrt{n} = u^{2329} $

where $ u = x'\_1 + y'\_1 \sqrt{4729494} $, and $ x'\_1 $ and $ y'\_1 $ have significantly fewer decimal digits (45 and 41 respectively). This representation drastically reduces the complexity of handling large numbers.

**Efficient Algorithms**

Methods related to the quadratic sieve approach for integer factorization can be adapted to exploit relations between prime numbers in the number field generated by $ \sqrt{n} $. These relations are combined to find a product representation, significantly enhancing the efficiency of solving Pell's equation compared to the continued fraction method.

Under the assumption of the generalized Riemann hypothesis, the complexity of this algorithm is:

$ \exp O(\sqrt{\log N \log \log N}) $

where $ N = \log n $ is the input size, analogous to the quadratic sieve algorithm for integer factorization. This indicates a sub-exponential time complexity, which is more efficient than the polynomial time complexity implied by the continued fraction method.

## Generalized Pell's Equation

The equation $ x^2 - dy^2 = N $ is known as the generalized Pell's equation, where $ d $ and $ N $ are integers. When $ N = 1 $, it reduces to the classical Pell's equation $ x^2 - dy^2 = 1 $. The equation $ u^2 - dv^2 = 1 $ is termed the Pell's resolvent.

**Recursive Algorithm and Solutions**

Lagrange provided a recursive algorithm in 1768 for solving the generalized Pell's equation, which simplifies the problem to cases where $ |N| < \sqrt{d} $. Solutions can also be derived using the continued-fractions method, similar to the classical Pell's equation.

If $ (x_0, y_0) $ is a solution to $ x^2 - dy^2 = N $, and $ (u_n, v_n) $ is a solution to $ u^2 - dv^2 = 1 $, then $ (x_n, y_n) $ given by:

$ x_n + y_n \sqrt{d} = (x_0 + y_0 \sqrt{d})(u_n + v_n \sqrt{d}) $

is also a solution to $ x^2 - dy^2 = N $. This principle is known as the multiplicative principle, where $ (x_n, y_n) $ is called a Pell multiple of $ (x_0, y_0) $.

**Finite Set of Solutions and Bounds**

There exists a finite set of solutions to $ x^2 - dy^2 = N $ such that every solution is a Pell multiple of a solution from that set. Specifically, if $ (u, v) $ is the fundamental solution to $ u^2 - dv^2 = 1 $, then each solution $ (x, y) $ satisfies:

$ |x| \leq \sqrt{|N|} \left( \sqrt{|U|} + 1 \right) / 2 $
$ |y| \leq \sqrt{|N|} \left( \sqrt{|U|} + 1 \right) / (2 \sqrt{d}) $

where $ U = u + v \sqrt{d} $.

**Applications and Uses**

Solutions to the generalized Pell's equation are instrumental in solving certain Diophantine equations and understanding units in specific rings. They are also relevant in the study of SIC-POVMs (Symmetric Informationally Complete Positive Operator-Valued Measures) in quantum information theory.

**Special Cases and Connections**

The equation $ x^2 - dy^2 = 4 $ is analogous to the resolvent $ x^2 - dy^2 = 1 $. Solutions to $ x^2 - dy^2 = 4 $ can be leveraged to generate solutions to $ x^2 - dy^2 = 1 $, particularly when $ d \equiv 5 \pmod{8} $, where every third solution to $ x^2 - dy^2 = 4 $ has $ x, y $ even, thereby generating a solution to $ x^2 - dy^2 = 1 $.

In summary, the generalized Pell's equation encompasses a broader class of Diophantine equations, with solutions and techniques derived from the classical Pell's equation, often involving algebraic and number theoretic methods to find and characterize its solutions.
